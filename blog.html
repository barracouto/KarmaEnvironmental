Best Management Practices

By Stephanie Couto
December 1, 2021

I think that most of us can agree that our daily 3:00 p.m. storms during the summer are relaxing, especially if you're just hanging out at home reading a book or catching up on a show. But those afternoon rain storms may be a little more sinister than you think. Stormwater is typically absorbed into the soil, which filters the water, and then eventually makes its way back into our aquifers, streams, and rivers. This is normally fine, but becomes concerning is when heavy rain water makes its way through the sewer drains carrying debris, chemicals, and harmful bacteria straight into our lakes, springs, and ocean. Shingles on a roof or pavements are impermeable surfaces and prevent precipitation from soaking into the ground directing it into drains and the sewer system. This can cause muddiness, flooding, blockages, and even infrastructure damage. Focusing on stormwater management will benefit our water quality by reducing the runoff of rainwater into the streets. Implementing green infrastructure captures stormwater and redirects it into natural hydrologies, restoring and maintaining them. You can start to help by reducing the amount of pollution that you produce at home and informing your neighbors, friends, and family about the importance of reducing their pollution. You may be wondering at this point, "what are some ways I can help reduce pollution?" Well, you can start with disposing your pet waste, safely using and disposing household chemicals, using and apply fertilizers and pesticides wisely, and using landscaping techniques that help prevent erosion. But the easiest way to help is to simply pick up trash when you see it. Click here for more information on Best Management Practices.

Is Nestle evil?

By Stephanie Couto
November 18, 2021

The purpose of a NPDES permit is to minimize the number of pollutants that construction and industrial sites dump into our water supply. An environmental resource permit regulates storage and management of surface waters, and pretty much is enforced to prevent stormwater pollution in natural bodies of water and helps to provide flood protection. An extraction permit allows a multi-billion-dollar company, like Nestle, to take 1.152 million gallons of water from an aquifer for a small one-time fee of only $115. Permits are normally enforced to help protect our environment and this is doing nothing but harming our water supply and monetizing it. But that's what Nestle does, they believe that drinking water is not a human right, promote bad health, and destroy our environment. White Sulphur Springs is a great example of over extraction. Some springs will dry up, some of them have dealt with harmful algae blooms killing wildlife, and other springs appear to have water intrusion. So, what is the point of even having a permit that just allows you to hurt our environment? Well, for the state of Florida they monetize off taxes paid by Nestle and the packaging facilities provide jobs. But will all of that be worth it in 70 years when our grandchildren can't enjoy a day at the springs, or when a 16-ounce bottle of water costs $10? At this point you are probably wondering what you can do to stop this. The first thing is to make sure you participate in local elections. Vote against any new proposition that allows corporations to take even more water for free and do not vote for any nominee that does not take the climate crisis seriously. Next, don't let your friends and family purchase any Nestle products and inform them on the horrendous business practices that Nestle implements. You can also support businesses like Marriott, who are implementing Smart Water Navigator which manages the amount of water that the company is using and developing a plan to minimize their water use. And finally, a super easy step is to buy a water filter and reusable water bottle so that you never have to buy a water bottle again.
